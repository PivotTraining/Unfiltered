Glow

Why turmeric works (and why most wellness shots fake it).

4 min read
G

Turmeric is famous. Curcumin is the molecule doing the work. And almost every shot on the shelf forgets the one thing that makes either of them useful.

The molecule, not the root

Turmeric the root is mostly water, starch, and fiber. The compound everyone's actually talking about is curcumin — a polyphenol that makes up roughly 2–5% of dried turmeric by weight. Curcumin is what the literature is studying when it talks about anti-inflammatory effects.

If you've ever taken turmeric on its own and felt nothing, this is why. The active molecule is a small fraction of what you're actually drinking.

The bioavailability problem

Even when you do get curcumin in, your body wants to dump it. It's poorly absorbed in the gut, rapidly metabolized in the liver, and quickly excreted. Studies estimate the bioavailability of curcumin on its own at well under 1%.

This is the part most shots quietly ignore. They list "turmeric" on the front and put no thought into whether your body can use any of it.

What black pepper actually does

Piperine — the active compound in black pepper — inhibits the liver enzymes that break down curcumin and slows its excretion. The result is a documented ~2,000% increase in curcumin bioavailability when the two are consumed together.

It's the difference between a shot that does what it says and a shot that's a placebo with branding.

What we do

Every Unfiltered Glow shot has fresh-ground black pepper. We bench-tested it at low, medium, and high concentrations and landed on the lowest dose that still hits the absorption window — enough to matter, not enough to taste sharp.

If a shot doesn't mention black pepper on the label, assume it isn't there. And assume the curcumin is going through you faster than you can blink.

One more thing

Fat helps too. Curcumin is fat-soluble. The trace coconut oil in our Glow recipe isn't there for flavor — it's there to ride alongside the curcumin through your gut. None of this is magic. It's just chemistry that most brands skip because it'd require them to think about a recipe instead of a marketing tagline.
